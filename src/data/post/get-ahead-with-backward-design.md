In todayâ€™s issue we present a step-by-step framework to get you started on your course creation, Backward Design of Your Cohort Based Course.

[Backward Design](https://cft.vanderbilt.edu/guides-sub-pages/understanding-by-design/) is an outcome focused research backed method of course design which starts from [Learner Outcomes](https://howtocbc.com/blog/learner-outcomes) and works backwards to figure out the steps necessary to achieve these outcomes. What this means is that curriculum content, assessment methods, and activities all follow logically from the [Learner Outcomes](https://howtocbc.com/blog/learner-outcomes) laid out at the start.


## Hello dear reader!

This [design methodology](https://files.ascd.org/staticfiles/ascd/pdf/siteASCD/publications/UbD_WhitePaper0312.pdf) is a natural consequence of applying [Learner Centred Teaching](https://newsletter.howtocbc.com/issues/howtocbc-pulse-pedagogy-of-cohort-based-courses-1046661) to instructional design. The emphasis is on creating the right conditions for students to achieve their outcomes.

Backward Design ensures that all elements of a course, from content to assessment to activities, coherently build up to learner outcomes. This keeps the course well-defined and efficient.

In addition, frequently gathering evidence throughout the course provides fast feedback loops. Instructors can continually improve and orient their teaching towards outcomes.

This stage starts with finding your niche area of expertise and a suitable audience that can benefit from it. Afterwards, you craft actionable learner outcomes for your course using the problems your students want solved. Check [our blog](https://howtocbc.com/blog/learner-outcomes) for a deep dive.

Stage 2: ğŸ” Define Acceptable Evidence of Learning

Identify practical evaluation criteria for students and instructors to measure outcomes. The [various types of evidence](https://uwm.edu/academicaffairs/facultystaff/assessment-of-student-learning/evidence-of-learning/) and metrics identified now can later be used to judge if the stated goals for the course are being met and to what degree.

For complex course level learner outcomes, the evidence may be long term tasks synthesizing various ideas learnt throughout the course such as a solo or group project. For an art course, it could be creating a work of art and writing a description to reflect their understanding. For smaller lesson level outcomes, the evidence can be smaller in scale such as case studies, in-class quizzes and polls, [minute papers](https://topr.online.ucf.edu/use-minute-paper-to-evaluate-student-participation/) ([sample](https://provost.tufts.edu/celt/files/MinutePaper.pdf)), or other short tasks.

There can also be independent evidence from third parties. For a computer programming course, such evidence can include taking part in a [public coding competition](https://www.analyticsinsight.net/top-10-upcoming-coding-and-programming-competitions-for-developers/).
